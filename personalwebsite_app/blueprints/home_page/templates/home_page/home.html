Hi! As you may have guessed, my name is Anthony Biel. I am a self-taught junior Machine Learning engineer who is passionate about the three pillars of the discipline, namely: Intelligence, Mathematics, and Computer Science. My motto is ∇Excelsior, the nabla symbol ∇ is a reminder of the gradient that supports the importance of working smart, and Excelsior (meaning "Ever Upward") supports the importance of consistency and discipline.

The fact that I didn't go to university makes me what we commonly call a "self-taught". According to the first definition that Google gives us when we search for "self-taught meaning", it corresponds to, I quote:

"having acquired knowledge or skill on one's own initiative rather than through formal instruction or training."

But this definition corresponds only partially to my personal adventure of self-taught. It is true that nobody forced me to start such an adventure, or nobody forced me to learn on a daily basis, and in this case we can indeed say that this process was based on my own initiative. But I think one of the main reasons that in the current world we link the self-taught to the dilettante is the second part of the definition: "rather than through formal instruction or training."

So it's true, I wasn't getting ready for college in the morning, but that didn't stop me from having a very structured schedule which has followed me for more than three and a half years at the present time (December 2022), dictating what and when to study, when to practice, when to eat, when to work out and when to sleep. My point is that the definition and the representation of the self-taught person in the social schema could lead one to believe that the self-taught person just learns what he or she wants to learn and when he or she wants to learn it.

So what I prefer to say in this case is that I studied at my own university.

First of all, I want to make it clear that I really like the traditional university and what it has to offer. The choice I made depended on various factors, including the different alternatives available to me, the focus of my studies which fit well with this choice and the fact that I am convinced of one thing: what really counts in the end is not a diploma but the value of the individual behind it, and to prove this value on the competence side not by paper but by concrete projects.

What I call my own university is an alternative way (which, in some cases, can make sense) to acquire some knowledge using only the internet and books.

In order to have a better visibility of what it was like for me, I will try to briefly define the major components of a traditional university and then try to project these different components into this alternative way of education.

I think the traditional university has four major components.

  • The first component is efficiency: given what the student wants to study, the university shows him a coherent path that, if well defined and followed, will allow him to acquire the necessary knowledge in the right order.
  • The second component is learning : given the path previously obtained made up of different blocks of knowledge, the university gathers for each of these blocks, the right tools, a pedagogical support as well as a teacher to deliver the content.
  • The third component is help : given a block of knowledge, the student can ask for help from the teacher or technical advisors.
  • The fourth component is sociability: being surrounded by interesting people with whom to have interesting conversations.

Now, let's come to the comparison.

  • For the first component, efficiency, the internet and its multiple resources can allow us to define this coherent path, and very often it is even possible to have an overview of the different blocks on this path with the reason of the chosen organization. Universities being often at the origin of the evolution of this path, by their force of discovery and their capacity to filter those of the others, it is also interesting to consult directly their syllabus in order to have information potentially still little democratized.
  • For the second component, learning, the desire of some to share their knowledge with new information and/or different perspectives and sometimes even trying to improve the transmission of information by using new technologies has made others happy, especially students like me. The ability to choose the person with whom we want to invest our time. The ability to evolve at your own pace, to go faster or slower when it makes sense, to be sure that you have mastered subject A before moving on to B which is based on A. The ability to choose what we consider the best for each block of knowledge, learning linear algebra with professor Pavel Grinfeld (MathTheBeautiful), probability with professor Joseph Blitzstein (Statistics 110: Probability), deep learning with professor Andrej Karpathy (CS231n), it has just been a wonderful experience.
  • For the third component, help, it is likely that in some cases, a student's request for help will be answered more quickly and appropriately in the case of the traditional university but I managed to turn this flaw into a strength, especially by learning to search for information, learning to ask better questions if I hope to get better answers, learning to sort out the information, learning to evaluate it by myself.
  • For the fourth component, sociability, it is true that my alternative loses a big point. While there is some form of sociability on the internet, and it is still possible to have/attend rich conversations, that for my part does not replace the quality of that experience at a traditional university.

This magnificent adventure also instilled in me discipline, perseverance, and efficiency in the use of my energy, hence ∇Excelsior, which is a personal symbolic representation of these lessons.
